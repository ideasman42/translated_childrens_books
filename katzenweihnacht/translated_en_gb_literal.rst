
###############
Cats' Christmas
###############


Page 1
======

| Novalis sat at the window and watched the snowflakes.
| They looked pretty, but he knew full well: When one
| caught them, they were first cold, then wet and then gone. Besides,
| one had to go out of doors for it, and there it was at present
| exceedingly cheerless. It was more fun, in the warm
| room to sit and to follow the whirling things
| with one's eyes.
| Tina came to stroke Novalis. A little impatiently
| he dodged aside. That people never noticed when a
| cat was otherwise occupied . . . Especially the very
| short people, as Tina was one.


Page 2
======

| “I'm writing a letter to the Christ Child,” said Tina to
| Novalis. “Because I wish for a whole lot of things, you see.
| And one must write them down for the Christ Child, otherwise
| it might possibly forget something.”
| Novalis listened attentively. That interested him.
| For he had wishes too.
| Tina took a piece of paper and began to paint blue signs
| upon it.
| Novalis would gladly have known who this Christ Child was. And
| where. And why it granted wishes. In any case it had to be
| rather clever, if it could understand the signs
| that Tina scribbled onto the paper.
| Novalis watched with his
| head cocked to one side.


Page 3
======

| I want to write a letter too, thought he. And he
| began to frame it in his thoughts.
|
| Esteemed Christ Child,
| if You really are as kind as is generally maintained,
| then I politely request of You the fulfilment of the following
| wishes:
| 1. No more locked doors in the house. I hate shut
| doors.
| 2. Fish more often for breakfast – or also for
| supper. I love fish.
| 3. The most important thing: send me a colleague. People
| are quite nice, but, when all is said and done, only people. And
| occasionally one wants to talk Cattish.
| He extends You the paw in greeting and thanks,
| Novalis, at present the only tom-cat here.
|
| So, thought Novalis. Now I need only bring signs onto
| a paper. He tried it with one of Tina's
| writing-pens. But the thing was not meant for cat's
| paws. It rolled over the table and fell onto the floor.
| Tina said something unkind to Novalis.


Page 4
======

| Offended, Novalis went into the next room. One of the
| big people sat there and was drawing black strokes
| onto a white paper. The black colour came out of a
| little pot, as Novalis observed. There was paper enough lying
| about too. Carefully Novalis dipped a paw into the pot
| and then set it onto white paper. “Decidedly
| beautiful,” he declared. “The Christ Child is sure to
| like that.”
| The loud, excited voice of the person startled him
| out of his occupation. “Leave that, you monster. Be off with you
| there! Of all things he must go at the ink-pot! This tom-cat costs
| me my last nerves!”
| Novalis fled and cleaned his black paw on the
| hall carpet. People! thought he, out of humour. They simply
| have no notion of anything.
| Grumbling, he withdrew beneath a
| sofa and tried to clean his
| still black paw with his
| tongue.


Page 5
======

| Upon a spacious cloud sat several angels and
| sorted letters. “The things that people all
| wish for!” said one of the angels, shaking his head.
| “Does anyone know what a computer game is?” cried another.
| “No idea,” said a third. “Never heard of it. When
| I was new here, the children wished for fairy-tale books and
| sweetmeats from the Christ Child. At the very most, once, a pair of warm win-
| ter shoes.”
| “Oh, what have we here!” One of the angels held up a
| white paper with black paw-prints. “The
| sender must be a cat. That does not happen often.
| Can anyone by chance read cat-writing?”
| “The Chief Post Angel, so far as I know,” cried someone.
| And so the letter with the black paw-tracks landed
| upon a rose-red express cloud that for the Chief Post Angel
| was intended.
| “Good Heavens, a letter from a cat!
| Such a thing I last had in my hands more than
| three hundred years
| ago,” growled the
| Chief Post Angel.


Page 6
======

| He put on a pair of gold-rimmed spectacles and studied for a
| while the black tracks upon the paper. “No
| chance,” he murmured at last, “this must be dealt with at the very highest
| quarter.” And he gave the letter to an Express-
| Angel who was just then flying by.
| The Christ Child was just then taking a pile of post out of the
| compartment marked “Impossible Things”. Quite by the by
| its glance fell upon the sheet of paper that the Express Angel
| had handed in. The Christ Child smiled . . .
| A little later the wish-list that Novalis had written
| lay in the department “granted”. Provided with the
| own-handed, most exalted
| signature.


Page 7
======

| Novalis was offended once again. They would not let him climb
| up the fir-tree that they had set up in the big room. They scolded be-
| cause the silver balls had all been broken to
| pieces. He had, after all, only tried out whether
| at least one could bounce. And the glitter-threads
| on the tree had made him dreadfully ill. Now he lay
| under the sofa and took it amiss.
| Christmas is silly, thought he. Never again shall I write the
| Christ Child a letter.
| The big people were stacking parcels round about the fir-
| tree. It rustled interestingly, and Novalis came out from
| under the sofa. But now again it did not suit them
| that he began to unpack. Although with his claws he
| could really do that superbly.
| “That is no tom-cat, that is a catastrophe,” said one
| of the people.


Page 8
======

| Novalis did not understand exactly what was meant by that. But
| that it was nothing friendly, even the thickest-skinned
| tom-cat noticed. And Novalis was not particularly thick-skinned.
| He went to seek comfort with Tina. The room door was
| shut once again. That too! And no one responded
| to his indignant complaint. By way of punishment he scratched at the
| fitted carpet. Then he lay down in a box beneath
| the big brick stove and resolved to sleep through
| Christmas. In cat fashion he did indeed fall asleep
| at once.
| Novalis woke at Tina's voice. “Novalis is gone.
| I can find him nowhere,” she complained. “Without him one
| simply cannot keep Christmas.”
| Novalis felt himself understood, yawned contentedly and came
| out of his hiding-place.
| “We shall read one more Christmas story, until it is quite
| dark,” said one of the big people.
| “Come and listen, Novalis!” cried Tina. “Stories are
| fine.” Very well, since it is Christmas, thought Novalis
| peaceably, and lay down beside Tina
| on the sofa.


Page 9
======

| The person with the deep voice began to read aloud
| from a thick book.
| The beginning of the story Novalis missed,
| because he was trying to catch a fly. But
| then he listened.
| It was all quite frightfully sad. Nowhere would
| they give Joseph and Mary a place to sleep and something to eat.
| And it was so cold outside. Novalis was not
| quite sure whether by Joseph and Mary people or cats
| were meant. But it made no difference either.
| Not even a person ought one to drive out in such
| weather! He shuddered at the thought of
| snow, cold and hunger.
| “Be merciful, let us in,” read the big person.
| Novalis pricked up his ears. Something was scratching at the
| door. “Be off with you, there is no room for you here,” read the
| person on. This time the noise at the door was not
| to be overheard. “That fits the story exactly,” said the
| person. He laid the book aside and went out to have a
| look.


Page 10
=======

| “Just look what was out there,” said the person, as he
| came in again. He set a shaggy, wet Something down upon
| the floor, which first of all shook itself vigorously
| and then sneezed three times.
| That could become a cat, once it dries, thought
| Novalis. He went closer, sniffing. The wet Something sneezed
| again and shrank back before Novalis.
| “Do you come from the Christ Child?” asked Novalis. “Don't know
| it,” said the Wet thing. “I had best be going again.”
| “Out of the question,” growled Novalis. “You are my
| Christmas present.”
| “I am cooking fish for the cats,” said the person with the
| bright voice. Another present, marvelled Novalis. Never
| again shall I grumble about Christmas.
| After a while the person with the bright voice came
| back and said to the person with the dark
| voice: “Have you noticed yet? In the whole house one
| can no longer shut the doors. They stick, or something
| of that sort.”
| So it is thorough. That one must really grant the Christ
| Child, thought Novalis.
