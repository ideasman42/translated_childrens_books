A Cat's Christmas
#################

Page 1
======

| Novalis sat at the window watching the snowflakes.
| They looked pretty, but he knew exactly what happened if you
| caught one: first it was cold, then wet, and then gone. Besides,
| to do so you had to go out of doors, and just then it was
| exceedingly unpleasant out there. It was much more fun to sit
| in the warm room and watch the whirling things.
| Tina came over to stroke Novalis. He moved out of her way,
| a little impatiently. People never noticed when a
| cat was otherwise engaged . . . especially the very short ones,
| like Tina.


Page 2
======

| “I'm writing a letter to the Christ Child,” Tina told Novalis.
| “Because there are ever so many things I want. And you have to
| write them down for the Christ Child, you see, or he might
| forget something.”
| Novalis listened attentively. That interested him. He had
| wishes too.
| Tina took a piece of paper and began drawing blue marks on it.
| Novalis wondered who this Christ Child was.
| And where. And why he granted wishes. In any case, he had to be
| rather clever if he could understand the marks Tina was
| scribbling on the paper. Novalis watched with his
| head cocked to one side.


Page 3
======

| I want to write a letter too, he thought, and began composing
| it in his head.
|
| Esteemed Christ Child,
| If you really are as kind as is generally claimed, I respectfully
| request that you grant the following wishes:
| 1. No more closed doors in the house. I hate shut doors.
| 2. Fish for breakfast more often – or for supper as well. I love fish.
| 3. Most important of all: send me a colleague. People are quite
| nice, but they are, after all, only people. And now and again one
| wants to speak Cattish.
| Offering you a paw in greeting and thanks,
| Novalis, at present the only tom-cat here.
|
| There, thought Novalis. Now I need only put some marks on a
| piece of paper. He tried using one of Tina's pencils, but the
| thing was not made for cats' paws. It rolled across the table
| and fell on the floor. Tina said something unkind to Novalis.


Page 4
======

| Offended, Novalis went into the next room. One of the big people
| was sitting there, making black strokes on a sheet of white paper.
| The black ink came from a little pot, Novalis observed. There
| was plenty of paper lying about as well. Carefully, Novalis dipped
| one paw into the pot and pressed it on to a clean sheet.
| “Decidedly handsome,” he declared. “The Christ Child is certain
| to like that.”
| The big person's loud, agitated voice startled him from his work.
| “Leave that alone, you little beast! Clear off! Of all the things
| to get into, it had to be the inkpot! This cat will be the death
| of me!”
| Novalis fled and wiped his black paw on the hall carpet.
| People! he thought crossly. They haven't the faintest idea
| about anything.
| Grumbling, he retreated beneath a sofa and tried to clean
| his still-black paw with his tongue.


Page 5
======

| Several angels sat upon a spacious cloud, sorting letters.
| “The things people wish for!” said one angel, shaking his head.
| “Does anybody know what a computer game is?” called another.
| “No idea,” said a third. “Never heard of one. When I first came
| here, children wished for books of fairy tales and sweets from the Christ Child.
| At the very most, they might ask for a pair of warm winter shoes.”
| “Oh, what have we here?” One of the angels held up a white sheet
| bearing black pawprints. “The sender must be a cat. That does not
| happen often. Can anybody read cat-writing, by any chance?”
| “The Chief Post-Angel can, as far as I know,” somebody called.
| And so the letter with the black pawprints landed on a rose-pink
| express cloud bound for the Chief Post-Angel.
| “Good heavens, a letter from a cat! I haven't held one of those for
| more than three hundred years,” grumbled the Chief Post-Angel.


Page 6
======

| He put on a pair of gold-rimmed spectacles and spent some time
| studying the black marks on the paper. “No chance,” he murmured
| at last. “This will have to be dealt with right at the top.”
| And he handed the letter to an Express Angel who was just then
| flying past.
| The Christ Child was taking a pile of post from the pigeonhole
| marked “Impossible”. His eye happened to fall upon the sheet
| delivered by the Express Angel. The Christ Child smiled . . .
| A little later, the wish-list Novalis had written lay in the
| “Approved” department, duly furnished with the Highest Authority's
| own signature.


Page 7
======

| Novalis was in a huff again. The big people would not let him climb the
| Christmas tree they had put up in the big room. They had scolded him
| because all the silver baubles were broken. He had only been
| finding out whether at least one of them could bounce. And the
| strands of tinsel on the tree had made him dreadfully ill. Now
| he lay beneath the sofa and sulked.
| Christmas is stupid, he thought. I'll never write to the
| Christ Child again.
| The big people stacked presents around the Christmas tree. The parcels
| rustled intriguingly, and Novalis came out from beneath the sofa. But once
| again they were displeased when he began unwrapping them, although
| he could do it exceptionally well with his claws.
| “That's not a cat, it's a catastrophe,” said one of the big people.


Page 8
======

| Novalis did not quite understand what that meant. But even
| the thickest-skinned cat could tell that it was not complimentary,
| and Novalis was not particularly thick-skinned.
| He went to Tina for comfort. Her door was shut yet again. As if
| that were not enough, no one paid the least attention to his
| indignant protests. To punish them, he scratched the fitted carpet.
| Then he lay down in a box beneath the great brick-built stove and
| resolved to sleep through Christmas. In true cat fashion, he fell asleep at once.
| Novalis awoke to Tina's voice. “Novalis has gone. I can't find him
| anywhere,” she complained. “We can't have Christmas without him.”
| Novalis felt that here, at last, was someone who understood him.
| He yawned contentedly and emerged from his hiding-place.
| “We'll read another Christmas story until it is quite dark,” said
| one of the big people.
| “Come and listen, Novalis!” Tina called. “Stories are lovely.”
| Oh, very well – seeing that it's Christmas, thought Novalis in a
| conciliatory mood as he settled down beside Tina on the sofa.


Page 9
======

| The big person with the deep voice began reading aloud from a
| thick book.
| Novalis missed the beginning of the story because he was trying
| to catch a fly, but after that he listened.
| It was all terribly sad. No one anywhere would give Joseph and
| Mary a place to sleep or anything to eat, though it was so cold
| outside. Novalis was not quite sure whether Joseph and Mary were
| people or cats. It made no difference. Even a human being ought
| not to be turned away in weather like that! He shuddered at the
| thought of snow, cold and hunger.
| “Have mercy, let us in,” read the big person.
| Novalis pricked up his ears. Something was scrabbling at the door.
| “Be off with you. There is no room for you here,” the big person
| continued. This time there was no mistaking the noise at the door.
| “Why, it fits the story perfectly,” said the big person. He put down
| the book and went out to see what it was.


Page 10
=======

| “Look what was outside,” said the big person when he came back. He set
| a shaggy, dripping something on the floor. It gave itself a
| vigorous shake, then sneezed three times.
| That might turn into a cat when it dries, thought Novalis. He
| went closer and sniffed. The wet something sneezed again and
| drew back from him.
| “Have you come from the Christ Child?” asked Novalis.
| “Don't know him,” said the wet creature. “I'd better
| be off again.”
| “Out of the question,” growled Novalis. “You are my Christmas present.”
| “I'm going to cook some fish for the cats,” said the big person
| with the high voice.
| Another present! marvelled Novalis. I'll never grumble
| about Christmas again.
| After a while, the big person with the high voice returned and said
| to the big person with the deep voice, “Have you noticed? None of
| the doors in the whole house will shut any more. They seem to be
| sticking, or something of the sort.”
| The Christ Child certainly does things thoroughly. You have to
| grant him that, thought Novalis.
