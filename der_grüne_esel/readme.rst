##############
Der grüne Esel
##############

*English translation "The Green Ass".*

:Retold by: Hans Baumann
:Illustrated by: Monika Laimgruber
:Published: 1989
:ISBN: 3-8067-4109-3

Seven old fables retold, after La Fontaine, Gellert & Aesop,
with one from Tibet, one from the Yiddish and one of Baumann's own.

Translation
===========

Following 1800-1900's English, British text (Grimms fairy tales style).
Using children's vocabulary but avoiding to overly simplify/dumb-down language.

- ``translated_en_gb_literal.rst`` keeps as close to the German as English allows
  (word order, thee/thou for the familiar "du") for reading against the original.
- ``translated_en_gb.rst`` is a freer version in the same period voice,
  refined over several passes: idiom, register, consistency across the fables & typography.

Both keep line numbers matching the original.


Notes
=====

"Esel" is rendered "ass" throughout, as in Victorian translations of Grimm and Aesop -
"donkey" would suit a modern register better.

The joke in "Das Blitzchen" hangs on stacked German diminutives
(Donnerwetterchen, Fritzchen, Blitzchen) which English can't reproduce directly;
the literal version calques them ("little thunder-weather...") and the free version
settles for "Bless my soul, little Fritz - what a flash of lightning that was!"

"Der Brunnentiger" is titled "The Well-Tiger" in the literal version
but "The Tiger in the Well" in the free one.
