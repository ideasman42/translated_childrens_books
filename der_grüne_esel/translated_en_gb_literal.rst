Page 1: **A Little Bell for the Cat**
=====================================

| Among the mice was no one more feared than the cat.
| So the mice held counsel, how they might guard themselves
| from this dreadful monster.
| An old he-mouse had a happy thought. “If we hang a little
| bell upon the cat, then can we hear her from afar,”
| said he. “Then the cat will catch not one mouse
| more.”
| “Bravo!” cried the mice. “That is good!”
| “But who will hang the little bell upon the cat?”
| asked a mouse-child.
| “Not I, not I!” said one mouse after the other.
| And the cat remained the terror of the mice.


Page 2: **The Green Ass**
=========================

| A man had an ass, of which he was very proud. It
| vexed him that not all held him for an uncommon ass.
| And so one day he painted him over with green paint.
| Only the ears and the legs did he colour red.
| When he went through the town with the green ass, the
| people came running together, windows were flung open, on many a
| hearth the dinner burnt. Every one wished to see the green
| ass, and all cried: “A green ass, how wonderful!”
| On the second day also there was still a throng of people
| wherever the green ass appeared. But three days later
| no one turned round any more after the green ass.


Page 3: **The Well-Tiger**
==========================

| The tiger had taken the fox into his service. Of
| the booty, however, he gave him very little. When the tiger had
| slain a beast, the fox must carry the pieces of flesh to the well
| and wash them clean. Of the best pieces he got
| nothing.
| One day the fox devoured a piece of flesh at the
| well, and came back with a wretched remnant.
| The tiger roared at him: “I gave thee a whole haunch
| to carry — where is it?”
| “The well-tiger has eaten it from me,” said the fox.
| “He will now have the greatest part of the booty every time!”
| “What manner of tiger should that be!” cried the tiger.
| “He looks like thee,” said the fox. “Only he dwells in the
| well — as thou in thy den.”
| Furious, the tiger ran to the well and looked in. And
| truly — a furious tiger looked back at him. The
| tiger roared and sprang upon the tiger in the well.
| And that was the end of them both!


Page 4: **Uphill and Downhill**
===============================

| Two wanderers once journeyed along a road on which
| it went unceasingly uphill and downhill. The one made
| a contented face going uphill — but going downhill he put on
| a sour countenance.
| “Thou goest, then, more gladly uphill than downhill?” asked the other
| in astonishment.
| “Quite the contrary!” gave the one for answer. “But going
| uphill I rejoice already at the going downhill, and downhill
| I think with dread thereon, that soon it goes uphill
| again.”
| Then the other laughed and said: “From thee may one learn
| something.”


Page 5: **The Little Lightning-Flash**
======================================

| Peter and Fritz were good friends. Only one thing troubled Peter in
| his friend: Fritz said not stone, but little stone,
| even when it was a great stone! And he said even
| little elephant instead of elephant.
| One day, when the two were on their way, they were overtaken
| by a thunderstorm. They took shelter by a barn.
| Then the lightning struck, and the barn began to
| burn.
| The two escaped with the fright, but Fritz
| could bring out not a word. Then said Peter:
| “Little thunder-weather, little Fritz, that was a little lightning-flash!”
| At that Fritz must needs laugh aloud. Yet from that day on he said
| no more little elephant, but elephant. And instead of
| little stone he said stone, when it was a stone in question.


Page 6: **Not Every One Is a Hero**
===================================

| A circus-master said to a man: “Thou canst earn thyself
| much money, if thou wilt take part in my grand
| turn. Thou needst only slip into the skin which I
| still have from my second lion, and carry out my
| commands.”
| “If it be so simple!” said the man, and that evening he appeared
| as a lion in the circus. He did all that the master
| required of him, scratched himself behind the ear and beat upon
| a kettle-drum. There was great applause.
| Then came the second lion in — with wild leaps. Then
| cried the first lion: “Do me no harm, lion, do me no harm!”
| “Never fear!” roared the second lion. “If only thou
| do me none!”
| The master cracked his whip three times, bowed
| before the public and said aloud: “My most honoured
| ladies and gentlemen! Most honoured children! Where is such a thing
| still to be found — speaking lions!!”


Page 7: **The Hares and the Frogs**
===================================

| Once the hares came together and lamented:
| “Sad, sad it is, that we must ever live in fear!
| All pursue us: the hunters with their hounds, hawks
| and foxes. Impossible to endure it any longer! Far
| better that we die of our own will, and that at once, than
| tremble yet longer for our lives! Let us now
| go together into the water!”
| And they set out for the near-lying pond, there to
| drown themselves. But at the pond’s edge sat a great many frogs,
| and when the hares came storming up, the frogs sprang
| into the water: splash, splash, splash!
| This the hares saw and heard. They started, and an old
| hare said: “Hold, friends! Others have just done what
| we would have done. So there are some who fare yet
| more wretchedly than we. Let us live on.”
