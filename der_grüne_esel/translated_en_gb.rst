Page 1: **A Bell for the Cat**
==============================

| Of all creatures, none was so dreaded by the mice as the cat.
| So the mice held a council to consider how they
| might keep themselves safe from the fearsome beast.
| An old he-mouse hit upon an idea. “If we were to hang
| a little bell about the cat’s neck, we should hear her
| coming from far off,” said he. “Then she would never
| catch so much as one mouse more.”
| “Bravo!” cried the mice. “A capital plan!”
| “But who is to hang the bell about the cat’s neck?”
| piped up a young mouse.
| “Not I! Not I!” said one mouse after another.
| And so the cat remained the terror of the mice.


Page 2: **The Green Ass**
=========================

| There was once a man who had an ass of which he was mightily
| proud, and it galled him that others saw nothing remarkable
| in it. So one fine day he painted the beast green from
| head to tail, save for the ears and legs, which he coloured red.
| When he led his green ass through the town, the people
| came running from every side; windows flew open, and many
| a dinner was left burning on the hearth. Everyone wanted
| a look at the green ass, and all cried: “A green ass — how marvellous!”
| On the second day, a crowd still gathered wherever
| the green ass showed itself. But three days after that,
| not a soul turned his head.


Page 3: **The Tiger in the Well**
=================================

| The tiger had taken the fox into his service, but of
| the spoils he gave him precious little. Whenever the tiger
| killed a beast, the fox had to carry the meat to the well
| and wash it clean — yet of the choicest pieces he got
| not a morsel.
| One day the fox ate up a fine piece of meat beside the
| well, and came back with only a miserable scrap.
| The tiger roared at him: “I gave you a whole haunch
| to carry — where has it gone?”
| “The tiger in the well ate it,” said the fox.
| “And now he means to take the lion’s share every time!”
| “And what manner of tiger might that be?” bellowed the tiger.
| “The very image of you,” said the fox. “Only he lives
| down the well — as you live in your den.”
| In a fury the tiger raced to the well and peered in. And
| sure enough — a furious tiger glared back up at him. The
| tiger roared and hurled himself upon the tiger in the well.
| And that was the end of them both!


Page 4: **Uphill and Downhill**
===============================

| Two travellers were once making their way along a road
| that ran forever uphill and downhill. Going up, the one
| wore a look of great contentment — but going down, he
| pulled a sour face.
| “So you would sooner go uphill than down?” asked the other,
| much surprised.
| “Quite the contrary!” came the reply. “But going
| up, I look forward to the going down; and going down,
| I dread to think that soon enough the road will climb
| again.”
| At that the other laughed and said: “A man might learn
| a thing or two from you.”


Page 5: **The Little Flash of Lightning**
=========================================

| Peter and Fritz were the best of friends. One thing only
| vexed Peter: Fritz never said stone, but always little stone —
| though the stone was a great big one! Why, he would even say
| little elephant instead of elephant.
| One day, when the two were out walking, a thunderstorm caught
| them unawares, and they took shelter beside a barn.
| Down came a bolt of lightning, and the barn burst
| into flames.
| The pair escaped with no more than a fright, but Fritz
| stood there speechless. Then Peter said:
| “Bless my soul, Fritz — what a little flash of lightning that was!”
| At that Fritz burst out laughing. But from that day forward
| he said elephant, and not little elephant. And when a stone
| was a stone, he called it a stone, and nothing less.


Page 6: **Not Everyone Is a Hero**
==================================

| A circus-master once said to a man: “There’s good money
| in it for you, if you’ll play a part in my star turn.
| All you need do is slip into the lion-skin I still have
| from my second lion, and follow my commands —
| nothing more.”
| “Well, if it’s as easy as that!” said the man, and that very
| evening he appeared in the ring as a lion. He did everything
| the master asked of him, scratched himself behind the ear,
| and banged upon a kettle-drum. The applause was tremendous.
| Then in came the second lion — bounding wildly. At that
| the first lion shrieked: “Don’t hurt me, lion, don’t hurt me!”
| “Never fear!” roared the second lion. “So long as you
| don’t hurt me!”
| The master cracked his whip three times, bowed low
| to the audience, and announced: “Most honoured ladies
| and gentlemen! Most highly honoured children! Where else in all the world
| will you find the like — talking lions!!”


Page 7: **The Hares and the Frogs**
===================================

| Once the hares came together, bewailing their lot:
| “Alas, alas, that we must live our whole lives in fear!
| All the world hunts us: the huntsmen with their hounds, the
| hawks, the foxes. We cannot bear it a moment longer! Better
| by far to die by our own choosing, and this very hour, than
| to go on trembling for our lives! Come, let us all
| go into the water together!”
| And off they set for the nearby pond, to drown
| themselves. But at the water’s edge sat a host of frogs,
| and when the hares came charging up, the frogs leapt
| into the pond: plop, plop, plop!
| The hares saw and heard it all. They stopped short, and an
| old hare said: “Hold, friends! Others have just done the very
| thing we meant to do. There are some, it seems, in a sorrier
| plight than we. Let us live on.”
