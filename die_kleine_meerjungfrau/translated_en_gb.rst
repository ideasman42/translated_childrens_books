| Far out at sea, the water is as blue as the petals of the loveliest cornflower,
| and as clear as the purest glass; but it is very deep—deeper than any anchor-chain
| can reach. Many church towers would have to be piled one upon another to rise from
| the bottom above the water. Down there dwell the sea-folk.
| |__| Yet you must not suppose that there is nothing below but bare white sand. No indeed!
| There grow the strangest trees and plants, whose stalks and leaves are so pliant that
| they stir at the least motion of the water, just as though they were alive. Fishes,
| great and small, glide amongst the branches, even as the birds up here fly through
| the air. At the very deepest spot stands the palace of the Sea King. Its walls are
| of coral, and its tall pointed windows of the clearest amber; but the roof is made
| of mussel-shells, which open and shut as the water ebbs and flows. A wondrous sight
| they are, for in every shell lie glimmering pearls, any one of which would be a great
| ornament in a queen's crown.
| |__| The Sea King had been a widower for many years, and his aged mother kept house for him.

----

| She was a wise woman, though proud of her noble birth; therefore she wore twelve
| oysters upon her tail, whilst the other noble folk were permitted only six.
| In everything else she deserved the highest praise, and especially for the love she bore
| the little sea-princesses, her son's daughters.
| They were six lovely children; but the youngest was the fairest of them all. Her skin
| was as clear and rosy as a rose-leaf, and her eyes as blue as the deepest lake; yet,
| like all the rest, she had no feet, for her body ended in a fish's tail.
| |__| The whole livelong day they played in the palace, in the spacious halls where real
| flowers grew out of the walls. The great amber windows were opened, and the fishes
| swam in to them, just as the swallows fly into our rooms when we open the casement;
| but the fishes swam straight to the little princesses, ate from their hands, and
| let themselves be stroked.
| |__| Before the palace lay a great garden, filled with trees of fiery red and deepest blue.
| Their fruit shone like gold, and their blossoms like flames of fire, for stalk and leaf
| were ever in motion. The ground itself was of the finest sand, but blue as
| the flame of burning sulphur.

| Over all things below rested a marvellous blue radiance. You might sooner have thought
| yourself high up in the air, with nothing but sky above and below, than at the bottom of
| the sea. In a calm the sun could be seen; it appeared a crimson flower, from whose
| cup streamed all the light.
| |__| Each little princess had her own plot in the garden, where she might dig and plant as
| she pleased. One shaped her flower-bed like a whale; another preferred hers to resemble
| a little mermaid; but the youngest made hers perfectly round, like the sun, and planted
| in it only flowers that glowed as red as the sun.
| She was a strange child, quiet and thoughtful. Whilst her sisters adorned their gardens
| with the strangest things gathered from wrecked ships, she wished for, besides the rose-red
| flowers that resembled the sun above, only one beautiful marble statue. It was
| a handsome boy, carved from pure white stone, and had sunk to the bottom in a shipwreck.
| Beside it she planted a rose-coloured weeping willow. It flourished splendidly, and hung
| its fresh branches over the statue and down towards the blue sand, where its shadow
| looked violet and moved with the boughs; it seemed as though tree-top and roots were at
| play, striving to kiss one another.
| |__| Nothing gave the little mermaid greater pleasure than to hear of the world of men above.
| Her old grandmother had to tell all she knew of ships and towns, of mankind and
| beasts. Above all, it seemed wonderfully beautiful to her that the flowers upon earth
| were sweet-scented—for those at the bottom of the sea had none—that the forests were
| green, and that the fishes seen amongst their branches sang so loudly and sweetly that
| it was a delight to hear them.

----

| They were the little birds; but the grandmother called them fishes, for otherwise the
| mermaids, who had never seen a bird, would not have understood her.
| |__| “When you have completed your fifteenth year,” said the grandmother, “you shall have
| leave to rise out of the sea, sit by moonlight upon the rocks, and behold the great
| ships passing by; and you shall see forests and towns also.”
| In the coming year the eldest sister would be fifteen; but the others—why, each was one
| year younger than the next, so the youngest had five whole years to wait before she
| might rise from the bottom of the sea and see what our world was like. Each sister,
| however, promised the one below her that she would tell all she had seen on her first
| day, and what she had thought most beautiful; for their grandmother could never tell
| them enough—there was so much they wished to ask about.
| |__| None was so full of longing as the youngest, who had the longest time to wait and was
| so quiet and thoughtful. Many a night she stood at the open window and gazed upward
| through the dark-blue water, whilst the fishes beat it with their fins and tails. She
| could see the moon and stars; they shone very faintly, it is true, but appeared through
| the water much larger than they do to our naked eyes. If something like a black cloud glided
| beneath them, she knew it to be either a whale swimming overhead or a ship bearing many
| men. They little thought that a sweet young mermaid stood far below, stretching her
| white hands towards their keel.
| |__| At length the eldest princess reached her fifteenth year and was permitted to rise to
| the surface of the sea.

| |__| When she returned, she had a hundred things to tell; but the most delightful, she said,
| was to lie by moonlight upon a sandbank in the quiet sea and look at the great town near
| the shore, where lights twinkled like hundreds of stars; to hear the music, the rumbling
| of wheels, and the clamour of men; to see the many churches and steeples, and to hear
| the ringing of the bells. And just because she could not enter it, she longed for the
| town more than for anything else.
| |__| Oh, how eagerly the youngest sister listened! Afterwards, when she stood in the evening
| at her open window and looked up through the dark-blue water, she thought of the great
| city with all its din and stir, and fancied that she could hear the church bells ringing
| even down to her.
| |__| The following year the second sister received leave to rise through the water and swim
| wheresoever she pleased. She reached the surface just as the sun was setting, and thought
| that sight the fairest of all. The whole sky had looked like gold, she said; and as for
| the clouds—ah, she could never describe their beauty enough! Red and violet they
| had sailed above her; but, swifter far than they, a flock of wild swans had flown like
| a long white veil across the water towards the setting sun. She too swam towards it;
| but the sun sank, and the rosy glow faded from sea and cloud.
| |__| The year after that, the third sister rose to the surface. She was the boldest of them,
| and therefore swam up a broad river which flowed into the sea. She saw glorious green
| hills covered with vines; castles and farmhouses peeped forth amid splendid woods. She
| heard all the birds singing, and the sun shone so warmly that she often had to
| dive beneath the water to cool her burning face. In a little bay she found a whole company
| of little human children. Quite naked, they ran into the water and splashed about. She wished
| to play with them, but they fled from her in alarm. Then there came a small black creature.
| It was a dog, though she had never seen one before.

| It barked at her so terribly that she grew frightened and hastened back towards the open
| sea; but she could never forget the magnificent woods, the green hills, and the charming
| children who could swim upon the water although they had no fish-tails.
| |__| The fourth sister was less daring. She remained in the midst of the wild sea, and declared
| that this was the most beautiful place of all. One could see for many miles around, and
| the sky above appeared like a vast glass bell. She had seen ships, but so far away that
| they looked like black-backed gulls; the droll dolphins had turned somersaults, and the great
| whales had spouted water from their nostrils until it seemed that a hundred fountains
| were playing on every side.
| |__| Then came the turn of the fifth sister. Her birthday fell in winter, and thus she saw
| upon her first visit what none of the others had seen.

----

| The sea looked quite green, and great icebergs floated all around. Each resembled a pearl,
| she said, yet was far larger than the church towers built by men. They appeared in the
| strangest forms and glittered like diamonds. She had seated herself upon one of the
| greatest, and every sailing vessel, filled with terror, sailed in a wide circle round the
| place where she sat, letting the wind play through her long hair.
| Towards evening, however, the sky became overcast; the lightning flashed and the thunder
| rolled, whilst the dark sea lifted the great masses of ice high upon its waves, so that
| they shone in the red lightning. On every ship the sails were furled, and fear and anguish
| reigned; but she sat calmly upon her floating iceberg, watching the blue lightning dart
| in zigzags into the gleaming sea.
| |__| Whenever one of the sisters first rose to the surface, she was enchanted with all the
| new and beautiful things she beheld.

| But when they were grown maidens and might go up whenever they chose, they cared
| for it no longer. They longed for home; and, when a month had passed, would say that
| their own home below was, after all, the loveliest, and that nothing was so comfortable
| as home.
| |__| Many an evening the five sisters linked their arms and rose in a row above the water.
| Their voices were glorious, more beautiful than any mortal voice; and when a storm was
| gathering and they believed that ships would sink, they swam before the vessels and
| sang sweetly of the beauty at the bottom of the sea, bidding the mariners not to fear
| coming down thither. But the sailors could not understand the words; they thought the
| voices were those of the storm, nor did they ever behold the splendours below; for when
| a ship sank, its crew were drowned, and reached the Sea King's palace only as dead men.
| |__| When the sisters thus rose of an evening, arm in arm, the youngest stood quite alone and
| watched them. It seemed as though she must weep; but a mermaid has no tears, and therefore
| suffers all the more.
| |__| “Oh, were I but fifteen!” said she. “I know that I shall love the world above, and the
| people who build their homes there.”
| |__| At last she was fifteen years old.
| |__| “Well, now we have brought you thus far,” said her grandmother, the old widowed queen.
| “Come, let me dress you like your sisters.” She placed upon her hair a wreath of white
| lilies, every petal of which was half a pearl, and caused eight oysters to fasten
| themselves upon the princess's tail, that her high rank might be known.
| |__| “It hurts so!” said the little mermaid.

----

| |__| “Yes; one must suffer for finery,” replied the old lady.
| |__| Oh, how gladly she would have shaken off all this magnificence and laid aside the heavy
| wreath! The red flowers in her garden suited her far better; but she dared make no change.
| “Farewell!” she said, and rose through the water, as light and bright as a bubble.
| |__| The sun had just set when she lifted her head above the surface; but all the clouds still
| gleamed like roses and gold, and in the pale-rose air the evening star shone clear and
| beautiful. The air was mild and fresh, and the sea perfectly calm. A great three-masted
| ship lay near; only one sail was set, for not a breath of wind stirred, and sailors sat
| everywhere amongst the ropes and upon the yards. Music and song resounded from the
| vessel; and as evening darkened, hundreds of coloured lanterns were kindled, until it
| seemed as though the flags of every nation waved in the air.
| The little mermaid swam close to the cabin window. Whenever the water lifted her, she
| could look through the polished panes and see a crowd of richly dressed persons; but the
| handsomest of all was a young prince with large black eyes. He could scarcely have been
| more than sixteen years of age. It was his birthday, and all this splendour was in his
| honour. The sailors danced upon the deck; and when the young prince came forth, more than
| a hundred rockets rose into the air. They shone as bright as day, so that the little
| mermaid was terrified and dived beneath the water. Soon, however, she put out her head
| again, and then it seemed as if all the stars of heaven were falling upon her.
| Never had she beheld such fireworks. Great suns wheeled round; splendid fishes of flame
| darted through the blue air; and every light was mirrored in the clear, tranquil sea.
| The ship itself was so brightly lit that the smallest rope—and, still more
| plainly, every person—could be seen.

| Oh, how handsome the young prince was! He shook the men by the hand, laughing and smiling,
| whilst the music sounded through the glorious night.
| |__| It grew late, but the little mermaid could not turn her eyes from the ship and the
| beautiful prince. The coloured lanterns were extinguished; no more rockets rose, and the
| cannon ceased to fire; but deep beneath the sea there was a humming and a rumbling.
| Meanwhile she sat upon the water, rising and falling with it, that she might still look
| into the cabin.

| waves rose higher; great clouds gathered; and lightning flashed in the distance. Ah, a
| dreadful tempest was approaching! The sailors therefore took in the sails. The great
| ship danced with furious speed over the raging sea, whilst the waters rose like immense
| black mountains, threatening to fall upon the masts.

| But the ship plunged like a swan between the lofty waves, and was borne up once more
| upon their towering crests. To the little mermaid this seemed a merry voyage, but the
| sailors thought otherwise. The ship groaned and creaked; the stout planks bent beneath
| the violent blows of the sea; the mast snapped in the midst as though it had been a reed;
| and the vessel reeled whilst the water rushed into her hold.
| The little mermaid now saw that they were in danger. She herself had to
| beware of the beams and shattered timbers tossing upon the water. For one instant it was
| black as pitch, and she could distinguish nothing; then the lightning made all so bright
| that she recognised everyone aboard. Each man staggered about as best he might. She sought
| above all for the young prince, and when the ship broke asunder she saw him sink into
| the deep sea. At first she was overjoyed, for now he was coming down to her; but then she
| remembered that human beings cannot live in the water, and that he could enter her
| father's palace only as a dead man.
| No, he must not die! So she swam amongst the drifting planks and beams, forgetting that
| they might crush her; dived far beneath the waves; and rose again amid the billows until
| at last she reached the young prince. He could scarcely swim any longer in that stormy
| sea; his arms and legs were failing him, his beautiful eyes closed, and he must have died
| had not the little mermaid come to his aid. She held his head above the water and let
| the waves bear them wherever they would.
| |__| By early morning the storm was past. Not a splinter of the ship remained in sight. The
| sun rose red and glowing from the water, and its beams seemed to bring life to the
| prince's cheeks, although his eyes were still closed.

----

| The mermaid kissed his high, beautiful brow and stroked back his wet hair. He looked,
| she thought, like the marble statue in her little garden. She kissed him once more and
| wished with all her heart that he might live.
| |__| Before her she now saw the mainland: lofty blue mountains, upon whose summits the white
| snow shone as though swans were resting there; and, nearer the coast, lovely green woods.

| Close at hand stood a church or convent—she could not rightly tell which, but it was a
| large building. Lemon and orange trees grew in its garden, and tall palms stood before
| the gate. Here the sea formed a little bay, smooth as a mirror yet very deep, extending
| to a cliff upon which the fine white sand had been washed ashore. Thither she swam with
| the handsome prince. She laid him upon the sand, taking especial care that his head
| should rest high in the warm sunshine.
| |__| Presently the bells rang in the great white building, and a number of young girls came
| through the garden. The little mermaid then swam farther out, behind some high rocks
| that rose from the water. She covered her hair and breast with sea-foam, so that no one
| might see her face, and watched to see who would come to the poor prince.
| |__| Before long a young girl approached. She seemed much frightened at first, but only for
| a moment; then she fetched others, and the mermaid saw life return to the prince. He
| smiled upon all about him; but he did not smile upon her, for he knew not that she had
| saved him. She became so sorrowful that, when he was led into the great building, she
| plunged sadly into the water and returned to her father's palace.
| |__| She had always been quiet and thoughtful, but now she became more so than ever. Her
| sisters asked what she had seen upon her first visit to the upper world, but she told
| them nothing.
| |__| Many an evening and many a morning she rose to the place where she had left the prince.
| She saw the fruit in the garden ripen and be gathered, and watched the snow melt upon the
| lofty mountains; but the prince she never saw, and therefore returned each time more
| sorrowful than before. Her sole comfort was to sit in her little garden and clasp her
| arms about the beautiful marble statue.

----

| It resembled the prince; but she no longer tended her flowers. They grew like a wilderness
| over the paths, and twined their long stalks and leaves amongst the branches of the trees,
| until the place was quite dark.
| |__| At length she could bear her sorrow no longer and confided it to one of her sisters. The
| others soon learnt the secret; but no one else knew it, save two mermaids who told nobody
| but their dearest friends. One of these knew who the prince was. She also had seen the
| festivities upon the ship, and knew whence he came and where his kingdom lay.
| |__| “Come, little sister,” said the other princesses. Then, with their arms about one another's
| shoulders, they rose in a long line out of the sea, near the place where the prince's
| palace stood.

| upon the water and the beautiful plants growing in the great basin.
| |__| Now that she knew where he dwelt, she came thither many an evening and many a night. She
| swam much nearer the shore than any of her sisters had ventured—indeed, she passed up
| the narrow channel beneath a magnificent marble balcony, whose long shadow lay upon the
| water. Here she sat and watched the young prince, who believed himself quite alone in
| the bright moonlight.
| |__| Many an evening she saw him sail with music in his splendid boat, from which banners
| fluttered. She peeped at him from amongst the green reeds; and if the wind caught her
| long silver-white veil and anyone saw it, he supposed it to be a swan spreading its wings.
| |__| Many a night she heard the fishermen, as they went upon the water with their torches,
| speak well of the young prince. Then she rejoiced that she had saved his life when

| bosom, and how tenderly she had kissed him; but of all this he knew nothing—he could not

| |__| She grew ever fonder of mankind, and longed more and more to rise amongst them.

| Their world appeared to her far larger than her own. They could sail over the sea in
| ships, climb the high mountains above the clouds, and possess lands whose forests and
| fields stretched farther than her sight could reach. There was so much she wished to
| know; but her sisters could not answer all her questions, and therefore she asked her
| old grandmother, who knew well the upper world, which she very properly called the lands
| above the sea.
| |__| “If men are not drowned,” asked the little mermaid, “do they live for ever? Do they not
| die as we do here below?”
| |__| “Yes,” replied the old lady, “they too must die, and their lives are even shorter
| than ours. We may live for three hundred years; but when we cease to live, we become
| nothing but foam upon the water, and have not so much as a grave amongst those we love.
| We possess no immortal soul and can never live again. We are like the green reed: once
| it is cut down, it can never grow green again. Men, on the contrary, have souls that live
| for ever, even after the body has returned to earth. Their souls rise through the clear
| air, up to the shining stars. As we rise out of the sea and behold the lands of men, so
| do they rise to unknown and glorious regions which we shall never see.”
| |__| “Why have we no immortal souls?” asked the little mermaid sorrowfully. “I would gladly
| give the hundred years that still lie before me, if only I might be human for one day
| and afterwards share in the heavenly world.”
| |__| “You must not brood upon that,” said the old lady. “We are far happier and better off
| than the people above.”

| |__| “Then I must die and float as foam upon the sea, never again hearing the music of the
| waves, nor beholding the beautiful flowers and the red sun! Is there nothing I can do
| to gain an immortal soul?”
| |__| “Nothing,” replied the grandmother, “unless a man should love you so dearly that you were
| more to him than father or mother; unless all his thoughts and affections were given to
| you, and he suffered the priest to join his right hand with yours, promising to be true to you, here
| and for evermore. Then his soul would pass into your body, and you too would
| share in the happiness of mankind. He would give you a soul and yet retain his own.
| But that can never be! Your fish's tail, which we think so beautiful in the sea, is
| considered ugly upon the earth. They know no better; there one must have two clumsy
| pillars, which they call legs, in order to be thought handsome.”
| |__| The little mermaid sighed and looked sorrowfully at her fish's tail.
| |__| “Let us be merry,” said the old lady. “We will leap and sport throughout the three hundred
| years that are granted us; surely that is long enough, and afterwards we may rest in our
| graves all the more contentedly. This evening there is to be a court ball.”

| shells, rose-red and grass-green, stood in rows on either side, each containing a blue
| flame that illuminated the entire hall and shone through the walls, so that the sea
| without was quite light. Countless fishes, great and small, could be seen swimming close
| to the glass; the scales of some glowed crimson, whilst others shone like silver and gold.
| Through the midst of the hall flowed a broad stream.

| Upon it mermen and mermaids danced to the music of their own beautiful singing. No
| mortals possess such glorious voices. The little mermaid sang sweetest of all,
| and the whole court applauded her. For a moment she felt joy at heart, for she knew that
| hers was the loveliest voice upon earth or sea.
| |__| But soon she thought again of the world above. She could not forget the handsome prince,
| nor her sorrow at not possessing, like him, an immortal soul. She stole out of her
| father's palace; and whilst song and merriment filled the halls within, she sat sadly in
| her little garden. Then she heard a French horn sounding through the water and
| thought, “He must be sailing above—he whom I love more than father or mother; he to whom
| my every thought clings, and into whose hand I would entrust the happiness of my life.
| I shall venture all to win him and an immortal soul! Whilst my sisters dance in my
| father's palace, I will go to the sea-witch, of whom I have always been so greatly afraid.
| Perhaps she can advise and help me.”
| |__| The little mermaid left her garden and went towards the roaring whirlpools beyond which
| the witch dwelt. She had never travelled that way before. Neither flowers nor sea-grass
| grew there; bare grey sand alone stretched towards the whirlpools, where the water spun
| like rushing mill-wheels and dragged everything it seized down into the depths. She had to
| pass through the midst of these crushing currents to reach the sea-witch's
| dominions; and for a long distance the only path led across hot bubbling mire, which the
| witch called her peat-bog. Beyond it stood her house in the midst of a strange forest.
| All the trees and shrubs were polyps, half animal and half plant. They looked like
| hundred-headed serpents growing out of the ground.

----

| Every branch was a long, slimy arm with fingers like wriggling worms; joint by joint
| they moved, from the root to the uttermost tip. Whatever they could seize in the sea,
| they wound themselves about and never let it go again. The little mermaid stopped outside in
| terror. Her heart beat with fear, and she had almost turned back; but then she remembered
| the prince and the immortal soul, and her courage returned. She bound her long flowing
| hair closely about her head, lest the polyps should grasp it; folded both hands upon her
| bosom; and flew, as only a fish can fly through water, between the hideous creatures,
| which stretched their pliant arms and fingers after her.
| She saw that each polyp held something it had caught, clasped fast by a hundred little
| arms like strong bands of iron. The white skeletons of men who had perished at sea peered
| out between their coils. They held fast rudders and chests from wrecked ships, the bones
| of land animals, and even a little mermaid whom they had caught and strangled. That,
| to her, was almost the most dreadful sight of all.
| |__| She now came to a great slimy clearing in the forest, where large fat water-snakes
| gambolled, displaying their ugly, whitish-yellow bellies. In the centre stood a house
| built from the white bones of shipwrecked men. There sat the sea-witch, feeding a toad from

| you shall have your way, though it will bring you to misery, my dainty princess. You wish
| to be rid of your fish's tail and to have instead two stumps upon which to walk like men,
| so that the young prince may fall in love with you, and that you may gain him—and an
| immortal soul besides!”

| At this the witch laughed so loudly and horribly that the toad and snakes fell to the
| ground and rolled about there. “You have come just in time,” she continued. “After sunrise
| to-morrow I shall not be able to help you again for a year. I shall prepare a draught
| for you. Before the sun rises, you must swim with it to land, seat yourself upon the
| shore, and drink it. Your tail will then divide and shrink into what men call pretty
| legs; but it will hurt you as though a sharp sword passed through your body. All who see
| you will say that you are the loveliest human child they have ever beheld. You will
| retain your graceful, gliding step; no dancer will move like you. But with every step,
| you will feel as though you trod upon a sharp knife and your blood must flow. If you will
| endure all this, I shall help you.”
| |__| “I will,” said the little mermaid in a trembling voice; and she thought of the prince
| and of gaining an immortal soul.
| |__| “But remember,” said the witch, “that when once you have received a human form, you can
| never become a mermaid again. You can never return through the water to your sisters or
| your father's palace; and unless you win the prince's love so entirely that, for your
| sake, he forgets father and mother, and has the priest join
| your hands so that you become man and wife, you will gain no immortal soul. The first
| morning after he marries another, your heart will break and you will become foam upon
| the water.”
| |__| “I will do it,” said the little mermaid, pale as death.

| |__| “But you must also pay me,” said the witch, “and it is no small price I ask. You have
| the sweetest voice of any creature at the bottom of the sea, and no doubt expect to
| charm the prince with it; but that voice you must give to me. I will have the best thing
| you possess in payment for my precious draught. I must put my own blood into it, that
| it may be sharp as a two-edged sword.”
| |__| “But if you take away my voice,” said the little mermaid, “what will remain to me?”
| |__| “Your lovely form,” replied the witch, “your gliding step, and your speaking eyes.
| With these you will still be able to bewitch a human heart. Well, have you lost your
| courage? Put out your little tongue, that I may cut it off for my fee, and you shall have
| the powerful draught.”
| |__| “Let it be so,” said the little mermaid; and the witch set her cauldron upon the fire
