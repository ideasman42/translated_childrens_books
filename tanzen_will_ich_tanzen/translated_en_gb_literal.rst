
###################
Dance Will I, Dance
###################


Page 1
======

| Mr Gonzolo was a puppet-player by trade. He had his own puppet-
| theatre. It was a little house with a red gable. Before the stage
| hung a red velvet curtain, quite as in a proper theatre.
| Mr Gonzolo possessed also a chest full of puppets. They were wondrously beautiful.
| The Princess Zelebrine had such long flaxen hair that it hung like a man-
| tle about her shoulders. Prince Radames wore a splendid turban of
| silk: red as cherries, white as milk and green as gooseberries. The
| robber Rumpo had boots that reached up to his belly, and a lan-
| tern that truly shone in the dark. But finest of all looked the Wild
| Man Hullabaloo: he had a golden ring in his nose and a
| fiery-red beard and eyes like an eagle-owl, which he could roll round about.
| When Mr Gonzolo played the Wild Man, he made his voice quite
| deep — ommm-brommm-pomm — till it sounded as though someone were grumbling in
| the deepest cellar, behind the apple-chests. But for the Princess Zelebrine
| he had to make his voice quite high and thin — tideli-wit-twit-twit-
| twit.
| At midday and in the evening the puppets were allowed to sing and dance upon the stage.
| But at night they hung by their threads in the dark behind the stage — like the
| bats — and slept.

======

| One day Mr Gonzolo received a little parcel by the post. It was stuck over and
| over with red postage-stamps, for it came from the puppet-factory in a
| far-off town. Mr Gonzolo tore the carton open at once with both
| hands. “Ha,” cried he, “at last it is here! This is surely the wondrously beautiful
| dancer that I ordered!”
| He turned the box upside down. Out fell, however, no dancer, but a
| little girl in a red frock. On her feet she wore little laced
| boots and on her head a black straw hat.
| Mr Gonzolo grew fearfully wrathful. His face turned scarlet. He
| squinted with rage! He waggled his ears! He bellowed so loud that the red
| curtain of the puppet-theatre fluttered as in a storm. “What impudence!” cried
| he. “I ordered a wondrously beautiful dancer, and what have they
| sent me? Whatever is that supposed to be?”
| “I am a little girl,” said the puppet with the red frock. “Can one
| not see that?”
| “A little girl? Hoho!” bellowed Mr Gonzolo, and seized the little
| puppet so hard about the body that he hurt her. “A little Schlumpi you are,
| that is all! Just look at yourself, you with your potato-nose!”
| The little girl had indeed a potato-nose.
| “And you have eyes like little bits of coal!”
| The little girl had indeed eyes like little bits of coal.
| “And you have a mouth from one ear to the other too!”
| The little girl had indeed a great mouth.
| “I should like best to pack you up and send you back again,”
| growled Mr Gonzolo. “But now you have once been delivered and paid for,
| so stay here for all I care!” And with that he seized the little puppet by her
| threads and hung her behind the stage with the others.

| In the evening the whole puppet-theatre was lighted with lanterns, and many
| people came to see the performance. The little puppet was quite
| excited. “At last I am out of my dark box!” cried she.
| “Now I shall sing and dance, and all the people will admire me!
| How I look forward to it!”
| She could scarcely wait till Mr Gonzolo took hold of her threads and fetched her
| forth from the dark room behind the stage. Her eyes shone.
| She was so glad! She would sing and dance, and the onlookers would
| clap rapturous applause!

======

| But Mr Gonzolo had something else in mind. He was still vexed that
| the puppet-factory had sent him a little unkempt girl instead of
| the beautiful dancer that he had ordered. Therefore he resolved that the
| Schlumpi-puppet should play the Punchinello. As she tripped out upon the
| stage and bowed, he pulled here and there upon the threads, and Schlumpi
| knew not what was happening to her. She slithered about as though she had ice beneath
| her feet. She lost her hat. She sat down upon her behind and stretched
| her legs in the air.


Page 5
======

| The people in the auditorium laughed. The puppets laughed too. “Heeheehee!”
| giggled Princess Zelebrine with her little silver-bell voice. “Haha!”
| laughed Prince Radames. “Hoho!” rumbled the robber Rumpo, and stamped
| with his cudgel upon the wooden floor of the stage. “Huaha-ha-ha —
| huaa!” bellowed the Wild Man Hullabaloo, and blew out his cheeks with laughing
| till he looked like a ship in full sail.


Page 6
======

| “Leave off laughing!” cried Schlumpi. “That is unkind!”
| But they all laughed still louder. The people in the auditorium laughed too.
| Schlumpi kicked her legs with rage. “Let me go!” she screamed at Mr Gonzolo.
| “Do not hold me fast! I will sing and dance! I know that I can do it!”
| But Mr Gonzolo spun her swiftly round, so that she tangled herself in all her
| threads and looked like a fly in a spider’s web. “There!” bellowed he.
| “And now dance the hop, that we may see what you can do!” And now
| he whirled the poor puppet round about by her threads till she grew quite
| giddy. He jerked so that she leapt high into the air. Then he let her fall,
| so that she crashed with her backside upon the boards. The people stamped
| their feet with laughing. The puppets laughed too. “Heehee! Haha! Hoho! Hua-
| ha-ha-ha!”

| “This Mr Gonzolo is truly a merry man,” said the people,
| as at last they went home. “We have seldom been so well enter-
| tained!”
| “You were screamingly comical once again, Mr Gonzolo!” cried the pup-
| pets in chorus. They flattered Mr Gonzolo at every opportunity.
| Only Schlumpi hung at the very end behind the stage by her threads and
| wept.


Page 8
======

| When the clock struck midnight, suddenly something came tripping along.
| Schlumpi opened her eyes and looked down. She saw a great,
| snow-white old rat sitting upon the boards below.
| “Ooh!” she cried, and tried to draw her legs up a little.
| “Just you leave my little boots alone!”
| Rats have respect for nothing, not even for marionettes. But the old
| rat said: “Forget your little boots. Are you the little girl who has
| wept the whole evening?”
| Schlumpi sniffled. “Yes,” she owned. “I wished to sing and dance! I wish-
| ed the people to take delight in me! I wished them to applaud
| me! But I am only a stupid marionette. I must play the Punchinello,
| he said.”
| “Fiddle-faddle,” replied the old rat, and flashed her red button-
| eyes. “Die one must, and naught besides. There, catch.” She sat up
| on her hind legs and threw the Schlumpi-puppet something round and golden.
| “That is for you. It is a magic amulet. It will fulfil your greatest
| wish.”
| “You mean it can change me into a real girl?” asked
| Schlumpi, astonished. The golden thing was heavy. It had points about its rim
| like a king’s crown. It shimmered in the darkness.
| “So it is,” said the rat. “And you shall sing and dance, and the people
| will clap as though they were mad. Yet there is a little catch. A
| magic spell belongs to it, you see.”
| “Tell it me, then!” cried Schlumpi urgently. “Oh, pray!”
| “That is just the catch,” said the old rat. “I cannot tell it you.
| No one can tell it you. You must find the magic word yourself. Well, you
| will manage it, surely. And now, good night!”
| And she vanished beneath the stage-boards.

| The other marionettes had listened to the conversation. Now they laughed
| themselves crooked. They believed not a word of all that the rat had said. “Well,
| have you found a magic word yet?” cried the Princess Zelebrine. “How
| would ROMMELPOTT do?”
| “Or perhaps BROMPIETZKY?” laughed Prince Radames.
| “Or SCHLUMZTRIMPLE?” bellowed the robber Rumpo. The Wild
| Man Hullabaloo said nothing. He only laughed. “HUA-WA-HA-HUA!”
| “Mr Gonzolo was truly right,” said the Princess Zelebrine.
| “This Schlumpi-puppet does indeed make us laugh again and again.
| She is a born Punchinello.”
| Schlumpi said nothing. She was far too furious to say anything. She only clat-
| tered her wooden eyelids and pressed the magic amulet to
| herself. Quite tightly. So tightly that it slipped into her frock and caught
| fast within. Now no one could see it any more.
| Not even Mr Gonzolo.

| But the other puppets told the old puppet-player what had come
| to pass. They all cried at once: “The Schlumpi-puppet means to run away! The
| Schlumpi-puppet was disobedient! The Schlumpi-puppet was impudent! You must
| show her once and for all who is the master in the house!”
| Mr Gonzolo bellowed with rage like a lion. He set all his hairs on end. He
| bared his teeth. And then he did something quite dreadful. He seized
| the little puppet so hard that her wooden body creaked and cracked. He
| pressed her arms to her body. He lifted her high. He tore open the stove-door and
| held Schlumpi right before the enormous fiery hole!
| “Aaaah!” shrieked the Schlumpi-puppet in horror. Flames leapt forth
| from the stove. Her little boots smoked. Her apron fluttered in the fiery
| wind. Her hair crackled. The tip of her nose grew glowing hot.
| “Hoho!” bellowed Mr Gonzolo, as he slammed the stove-door shut again.
| “Well, how did you like that, you stupid blockhead? That you may know it, you
| Schlumpi, you belong to me! And if you grow impudent, I shall break you
| to pieces and throw you into the stove!”
| Schlumpi trembled with fear. But she trembled with fury too. “I am no
| blockhead!” she cried aloud. “Neither am I your Schlumpi! I do not belong
| to you! I AM MY OWN SELF!”
| And that was the very magic word!
| So simple it was. She needed only to say: I AM MY OWN SELF.
| “I am running away, out into the wide world!” cried Schlumpi. And in her rage
| she struck Mr Gonzolo hard upon the nose. Very hard. His nose at once turned
| orange-red and grew as big as a pumpkin.

| Mr Gonzolo bellowed and cursed. Schlumpi ran away as fast as she
| could. Her hair fluttered in the wind. She had to hold fast to her hat. Mr
| Gonzolo could reach her no more.
| She was free!
| She leapt and danced. It was glorious to be a real little girl!

| “Hallo!” said a voice. It was the fat white rat. She said: “Well, what
| does one say when one has been helped?”
| “I beg your pardon,” said Schlumpi quickly. “I was uncivil. One says
| ‘Thank you’. Many, many thanks.” She lifted the rat up and kissed her on the
| snout. “Many thanks for your magic amulet.”
| “Brrrr,” went the rat, and wiped her snout. “Ugh. I hate
| being licked all over! — By the by, I must tell you something . . . about
| this magic amulet.”
| “What, then?” asked Schlumpi.
| “Well . . . er,” said the rat, “it was not a magic amulet at all, you see. It
| was only an old beer-bottle cap that I once found behind the house.
| But I thought it would give you courage.”
| “It did give me courage,” said Schlumpi.
| The rat was content. “Now, what say you?” she asked then. “Shall we
| run out into the wide world now and become rich and famous?”
| “Very well,” said Schlumpi.
