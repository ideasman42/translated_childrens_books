| Master Gonzolo was a puppet-showman by trade. He had his own puppet-
| theatre. It was a little house with a red gable. Before the stage
| hung a curtain of red velvet, for all the world like a real theatre.
| Master Gonzolo also had a chest full of puppets. They were wondrously fair.
| The Princess Zelebrine had hair so long and golden that it hung like a man-
| tle about her shoulders. Prince Radames wore a splendid turban of
| silk: red as cherries, white as milk, and green as gooseberries. The
| robber Rumpo had boots that came up to his belly, and a lan-
| tern that gave a true light in the dark. But the Wild Man Hullabaloo looked
| the finest of all: he had a golden ring in his nose, and a
| fiery-red beard, and eyes like an eagle-owl’s that he could roll round and round.
| When Master Gonzolo played the Wild Man, he made his voice quite
| deep — ommm-brommm-pomm — till it sounded as if some one were grumbling in the
| deepest cellar, behind the apple-boxes. But for the Princess Zelebrine
| he had to make his voice quite high and thin — tideli-wit-twit-twit-
| twit.
| At noon and in the evening the puppets might sing and dance upon the stage.
| But at night they hung by their strings in the dark behind the stage — like
| bats — and slept.


Page 2
======

| One day Master Gonzolo received a little parcel in the post. It was stuck all
| over with red postage-stamps, for it came from the puppet-factory in a
| far-off city. With both hands Master Gonzolo at once tore the box
| open. “Ha,” he cried, “here it is at last! This is surely the beautiful
| dancer that I ordered!”
| He turned the box upside down. But no dancer fell out — only a
| little girl in a red frock. Upon her feet she wore little laced
| boots, and upon her head a black straw hat.
| Master Gonzolo flew into a fearful passion. His face flushed scarlet. He
| went cross-eyed with rage! He waggled his ears! He bellowed so loud that the red
| curtain of the puppet-theatre fluttered as if in a storm. “What impudence!”
| he cried. “I ordered a beautiful dancer, and what have they
| sent me? What queer thing is this meant to be?”
| “I am a little girl,” said the puppet in the red frock. “Can
| one not see it?”
| “A little girl? Ho-ho!” bellowed Master Gonzolo, and he gripped the little
| puppet so hard about the body that he hurt her. “You are a little Scruffkin,
| that is all! Just look at yourself, you with your potato-nose!”
| The little girl had indeed a potato-nose.
| “And you have eyes like little lumps of coal!”
| The little girl had indeed eyes like little lumps of coal.
| “And you have a mouth, too, that goes from one ear to the other!”
| The little girl had indeed a large mouth.
| “I should like nothing better than to pack you up and send you back again,”
| growled Master Gonzolo. “But you are delivered and paid for now,
| so stay here, for all I care!” And with that he took the little puppet by her
| strings and hung her up behind the stage with the others.


Page 3
======

| In the evening the whole puppet-theatre was lit up with lanterns, and many
| people came to see the show. The little puppet was all
| in a flutter. “At last I am out of my dismal box!” she cried.
| “Now I shall sing and dance, and all the people will admire me!
| Oh, how I do look forward to it!”
| She could scarcely wait for Master Gonzolo to reach for her strings and
| fetch her out of the dark room behind the stage. Her eyes shone.
| She was so glad! She would sing and dance, and the audience would
| clap their hands in delight!


Page 4
======

| But Master Gonzolo had other designs. He was still vexed that
| the puppet-factory had sent him a little tangle-haired girl instead of
| the beautiful dancer he had ordered. So he resolved that the
| Scruffkin-puppet should play the fool Punchinello. As she came tripping out upon the
| stage and made her bow, he tugged at the strings, now here, now there, and Scruffkin
| knew not what befell her. She slithered about as though she had ice beneath
| her feet. She lost her hat. She plumped down upon her rump and stretched
| her legs up in the air.


Page 5
======

| The people in the pit laughed. The puppets laughed too. “Tee-hee-hee!”
| tittered Princess Zelebrine in her little silver-bell voice. “Ha-ha!”
| laughed Prince Radames. “Ho-ho!” rumbled the robber Rumpo, and thumped
| his cudgel upon the wooden floor of the stage. “Huaha-ha-ha —
| huaa!” bellowed the Wild Man Hullabaloo, and he blew his cheeks out with
| laughter, till he looked like a ship in full sail.


Page 6
======

| “Stop laughing!” cried Scruffkin. “For shame!”
| But they laughed all the louder. The people in the pit laughed too.
| Scruffkin kicked and struggled in her fury. “Let me go!” she shrieked at Master Gonzolo.
| “Do not hold me fast! I will sing and dance! I know that I can!”
| But Master Gonzolo twirled her swiftly round, so that she got tangled in all her
| strings and looked like a fly in a spider’s web. “There!” he roared.
| “And now dance us a caper, so that we may see what you can do!” And then
| he whirled the poor puppet round and round upon her strings, till she was quite gid-
| dy. He jerked the strings, so that she sprang high into the air. Then he let her fall,
| so that she crashed down upon the boards on her behind. The people stamped
| their feet with laughter. The puppets laughed too. “Tee-hee! Ha-ha! Ho-ho! Hua-
| ha-ha-ha!”

| “This Master Gonzolo is indeed a merry fellow,” said the people,
| as they went home at last. “We have seldom been so well enter-
| tained!”
| “You were roaringly comical again, Master Gonzolo!” cried the pup-
| pets in chorus. They flattered Master Gonzolo upon every occasion.
| Only Scruffkin hung by her strings, right at the end behind the stage, and
| wept.


Page 8
======

| When the clock struck midnight, something suddenly came pattering along.
| Scruffkin opened her eyes and looked down. She saw a great,
| snow-white old rat sitting upon the boards below.
| “Mercy!” she cried, and tried to draw her legs up a little.
| “Mind you leave my little boots in peace!”
| Rats have respect for nothing, not even for marionettes. But the old
| rat said: “Never you mind your little boots. Are you the little girl who has
| wept all evening long?”
| Scruffkin sniffed. “Yes,” she admitted. “I wanted to sing and dance! I want-
| ed the people to take delight in me! I wanted them to ap-
| plaud me! But I am only a foolish marionette. I must play Punchinello,
| so he said.”
| “Fiddlesticks,” replied the old rat, and flashed her red button-
| eyes. “Must, says he! There is only one thing you must do in this world, and
| that is die — nothing else at all. Here, catch!” She sat up on her hind
| legs and threw the Scruffkin-puppet something round and golden. “That is
| for you. It is a magic amulet. It will fulfil your greatest wish.”
| “You mean it can turn me into a real girl?” asked
| Scruffkin, astonished. The golden thing was heavy. It had points round its edge
| like a king’s crown. It glimmered in the darkness.
| “Quite so,” said the rat. “And you shall sing and dance, and the people
| will clap as if bewitched. To be sure, there is one small hitch. There
| is a magic spell that goes with it, you see.”
| “Do tell me!” cried Scruffkin beseechingly. “Oh, please do!”
| “That is just the hitch,” said the old rat. “I cannot tell it to you.
| No one can tell it to you. You must find the magic word for yourself. Well, you
| will manage it, never fear. But now — good night!”
| And she vanished beneath the boards of the stage.

| The other marionettes had listened to all this talk. Now they laughed fit
| to split their sides. They did not believe a word of what the rat had said. “Well,
| have you found a magic word yet?” cried the Princess Zelebrine. “What
| do you say to ROMMELPOTT?”
| “Or perhaps to BROMPIETZKY?” laughed Prince Radames.
| “Or to SCRUFFLETRUMPLE?” bellowed the robber Rumpo. The Wild
| Man Hullabaloo said nothing. He only laughed. “HUA-WA-HA-HUA!”
| “Master Gonzolo was right after all,” said the Princess Zelebrine.
| “This Scruffkin-puppet does indeed set us laughing again and again.
| She is a born Punchinello.”
| Scruffkin said nothing. She was far too angry to say anything. She only
| clacked her wooden eyelids, and pressed the magic amulet to her breast.
| She held it fast — so hard that it slipped down inside her frock and re-
| mained hanging there. Now no one could see it any more.
| Not even Master Gonzolo.

| But the other puppets told the old puppet-showman what had happened.
| They all cried out at once: “The Scruffkin-puppet means to run away! The
| Scruffkin-puppet was disobedient! The Scruffkin-puppet was impudent! You
| must show her once and for all who is master in the house!”
| Master Gonzolo roared with rage like a lion. He made all his hair stand on end. He
| bared his teeth. And then he did something quite dreadful. He seized
| the little puppet so hard that her wooden body creaked and cracked. He
| pressed her arms to her body. He lifted her up. He tore open the oven door and
| held Scruffkin right before the huge fiery hole!
| “Aaaah!” shrieked the Scruffkin-puppet in terror. Flames burst out of the
| oven. Her little boots smoked. Her apron fluttered in the fiery
| wind. Her hair crackled. The tip of her nose grew red-hot.
| “Ho-ho!” bellowed Master Gonzolo, as he clapped the oven door shut again.
| “Well, how did you like that, you stupid blockhead? And just you remember, you
| Scruffkin — you belong to me, do you hear? And if you grow insolent, I shall smash you
| all to pieces and throw you into the oven!”
| Scruffkin trembled with fear. But she trembled still more with rage. “I am no
| blockhead!” she cried aloud. “Nor am I your Scruffkin! I do not belong
| to you! I AM MY OWN SELF!”
| And that very thing was the magic word!
| So simple it was. She had only to say: I AM MY OWN SELF.
| “I will run away, out into the wide world!” cried Scruffkin. And in her rage
| she struck Master Gonzolo hard upon the nose. Very hard. His nose at once grew
| orange-red and as big as a pumpkin.

| Master Gonzolo bellowed and cursed. Scruffkin ran away as fast as ever she
| could. Her hair streamed in the wind. She had to hold on to her hat. Master
| Gonzolo could not catch her now.
| She was free!
| She leapt and danced. It was glorious to be a real little girl!

| “Hullo!” said a voice. It was the fat white rat. She said: “Well, and what
| does one say when one has been helped?”
| “I beg your pardon,” said Scruffkin quickly. “I forgot my manners. One says
| ‘Thank you.’ Many, many thanks.” She lifted the rat up and kissed her on the
| nose. “Many thanks for your magic amulet.”
| “Brrrr,” went the rat, and wiped her nose. “Pah! I do hate
| being licked! — By the by, I must tell you something . . . about this
| magic amulet.”
| “What is it?” asked Scruffkin.
| “Well . . . ahem,” said the rat, “that was no magic amulet at all, you see. It
| was only an old beer-bottle top that I once found behind the house.
| But I thought it would put heart into you.”
| “It did put heart into me,” said Scruffkin.
| The rat was well pleased. “Now, what do you say?” she asked presently. “Shall we
| run out into the wide world now, and become rich and famous?”
| “Very well,” said Scruffkin.
